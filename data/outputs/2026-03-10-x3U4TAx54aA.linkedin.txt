The housing correction is no longer a Toronto-only story.

It is becoming a broader capital allocation story across Canadian real estate.

What stands out most right now is how many pressure points are hitting at once: lower sales, weaker prices, softer rental conditions in key pockets, and a development pipeline that is still delivering supply into a slower market. Toronto is already down roughly 25% to 26% from peak by this analysis, Vancouver is deteriorating faster, and Alberta has moved from undersupply to oversupply much faster than many expected.

That combination matters because weak demand today and weak construction tomorrow can exist at the same time. A large wave of rental completions is still arriving, including substantial purpose-built rental supply in BC, while new single-family construction is sitting near multi-decade lows. Near term, that keeps pressure on pricing and absorption. Longer term, it raises the risk of another supply imbalance once the cycle turns.

There is also a macro layer the market cannot ignore. Financing conditions remain fragile, household formation is less predictable, and higher energy prices could keep rate risk alive even in a softer economy. In other words, falling prices alone do not guarantee a clean or quick reset.

For investors, builders, lenders, and operators, this is the key question: are we watching a normal correction, or a longer transition where multiple imbalances need to clear at once?

My view is that the market still looks heavy because those imbalances are not fully resolved yet.
