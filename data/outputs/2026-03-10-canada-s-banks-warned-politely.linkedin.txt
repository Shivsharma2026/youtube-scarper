The bigger risk in Canadian real estate may not be falling prices.

It may be the way risk is being carried while prices are falling.

One issue that deserves far more attention is the use of blanket appraisals on pre-construction condos. If financing is being supported by original purchase prices instead of current market value, then the market is not clearing on true economics. It is being stabilized by assumptions that may no longer hold.

That matters because loan-to-value discipline is supposed to be one of the core guardrails in mortgage lending. If a condo was bought for roughly $1.62M, is worth closer to $1.2M at closing, and financing is still being advanced off the higher figure, the risk has not disappeared. It has simply been repositioned.

The short-term benefit is obvious: more deals close, fewer projects face immediate disruption, and less stress shows up on the surface. But the long-term cost is credibility. Markets function when valuations, underwriting, and regulation remain aligned, especially during a downturn.

What makes this more serious is the broader signal it sends. If market participants believe downside risk will be softened through quiet flexibility rather than transparent repricing, leverage stays in the system longer and price discovery gets weaker.

For lenders, developers, investors, and policymakers, that is the real issue. Not just whether transactions keep moving, but whether the market is still being forced to tell the truth.
